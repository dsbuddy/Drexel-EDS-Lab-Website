Watershed Modelling

Watershed modeling refers to the use of computer software to evaluate the quantities and timing of water transport and associated pollutant loads (e.g., nitrogen, phosphorus, and sediment) that occur within a specified geographic area (typically a topographically-defined watershed) based on climate, landscape conditions, land use patterns and other factors over short-term (days, weeks, months) and/or long-term (years, decades) time frames. While some modeling approaches may utilize simple routines based on empirically-derived loading rates found in the literature (e.g., representative estimates of pounds per acre of nitrogen or phosphorus for different land cover types such as cropland or urban land), other more complex approaches employ detailed sub-routines specifically designed to simulate physical, chemical and biological processes that occur within a given area as affected by such factors as precipitation patterns, soil type, topographic slope, percent of impervious surface, animal populations, and fertilizer rates.

In the latter case where more detailed analyses are performed, accurate simulation of various processes requires that fairly precise estimates of key model parameters be made based on local conditions (for example, soil erodibility as affected by soil texture and land slope or infiltration of rainfall as affected by soil porosity and vegetative cover). In years past, such parameter estimates were primarily based on “best professional judgement” guided by the use of available paper maps such as USGS topographic maps and soil maps published by the Natural Resources Conservation Service. Over the last few decades, however, such model parameterization has been greatly enhanced via the use of geographic information system (GIS) software that allows fast combination and analysis of disparate digital datasets. In fact, most modern “integrated” watershed modeling packages now provide a GIS-based “front-end” to estimate values for key model parameters that are subsequently passed to an imbedded watershed simulation model. Such integrated packages also typically provide users with extensive capabilities for viewing model output.

In addition to simulating hydrology and pollutant loads, more robust watershed modeling approaches also include a capability to estimate potential pollutant load reductions that might occur as a result of the implementation of various best management practices (BMPs) such as conservation tillage, urban systems that capture and treat stormwater, riparian forest buffers and waste treatment systems for farm animal populations. Such a capability affords users the ability to iteratively compare potential load reductions against target thresholds designed to meet acceptable water quality goals.

In years past, modeling applications such as those described above were primarily developed for use on stand-alone desktop computers. With the advent of more advanced web-based software tools, however, it is now much more commonplace to develop on-line platforms to support such modeling activities. It is in this latter environment EDS has extensive expertise. Over the last few years EDS has been deeply involved in the development of modeling algorithms, data processing routines, and Application Programming Interfaces (APIs) for various large-scale, nationally-recognized modeling applications such as Model My Watershed ( modelmywatershed.org ) and the Stream Reach Assessment Tool for the Delaware River Watershed ( streamreachtools.org ). EDS is also currently working under a subcontract with the Chesapeake Conservancy to develop more advanced spatial modeling tools that enhance one’s ability to evaluate BMP-based pollutant load reductions at smaller scales using output from EPA’s Chesapeake Bay model in combination with high-resolution datasets and very fast spatial data processing tools.
